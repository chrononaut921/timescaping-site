THE MOMENT WE'RE IN

THE WORLD IS CHANGING FASTER THAN WE CAN MAKE SENSE OF IT TOGETHER.

That is not a metaphor. The systems we live inside are transforming at a pace that outstrips our collective ability to process what is happening, let alone imagine what comes next.

This creates a specific kind of disorientation. Not lack of information. Not lack of intelligence. A lag. Between how fast things move and how slowly humans integrate change emotionally, ethically, imaginatively.

We reach for familiar responses. We extrapolate the present. We collapse complexity into doom or inevitability. Not because we are passive. Because we don't feel permission to explore another way.

THE PRACTICE

100 YEARS
FORWARD.
TOGETHER.

Participants step 100 years into the future together. Using scenario cards built around specific themes, from android rights to climate restoration to the meaning of work, participants engage in conversations with each other and learn how to build nuanced speculative scenarios.

The 100-year distance is a device. Far enough that nobody has the right answer. Far enough that expertise stops being a shield. Far enough that the stories we tell ourselves about what is inevitable start to loosen.

WHAT HAPPENS WHEN PEOPLE IMAGINE TOGETHER

SIT TWO PEOPLE
WITH THE SAME SCENARIO.
THEY WILL NOT REACT
TO IT THE SAME WAY.
THAT IS WHERE A
SHARED NARRATIVE STARTS.

What we imagine reveals who we are now. What we fear. What we value. What we cannot yet conceive. Most people, asked to picture the future, land on doom or flying cars. Timescaping opens something in between.

People discover assumptions they didn't know they were making.

They find unexpected common ground with someone with a different worldview.

They leave looking at the present differently.

They catch a glimpse of a future they might actually want to create.

That is where vision starts.

THE PERSON IN THE ROOM

CRISTIAN
BOSCHERI

Cristian Boscheri is a former engineer and theater maker who has spent twenty years working across very different worlds. He also founded and ran an organization for nearly a decade.

That range is why he designs experiences that take imagination seriously, without losing touch with what actually happens in a room.

In the room, he knows how to read a group, hold it, and take it somewhere it wouldn't go on its own.
