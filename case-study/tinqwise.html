Thirty minutes. An online quarterly meeting. A question about whether future-thinking could happen that fast.

The organisation

TINQWISE

TinQwise is a digital learning company working at the intersection of AI and workplace development. The session was organised during their quarterly company meeting, with a theme built around the Retail Academy of the Future.

The audience was mostly senior roles with a digital learning and AI orientation, people who think carefully about how technology changes the way organisations grow and develop their people.

The Timescaping session

ONLINE,
30 MINUTES

The session ran for 30 minutes online as part of the quarterly meeting. Given the time constraints, the format was compressed but followed the core Timescaping protocol: a brief calibration exercise, paired card conversations, and a light closing.

Cards were selected from the standard deck and oriented toward the company's context in digital learning and the future of retail.

Preparation

TIGHT FIT,
RIGHT FIT

Cards were selected to align with the Retail Academy theme and the senior, AI-oriented audience. The session was kept tight to fit within the meeting structure without displacing the rest of the agenda.

Outcomes

OPENING
THE MIND

The session was received well. It felt aligned with the company's goals and context. No one felt uncomfortable or forced. Participants appreciated doing something different together as a team.

The CEO noted that opening the mind is important. That framing, simple and direct, matched what the session was designed to do.

'Loved the high pace energy and can-do forward thinking.'

— Emil de Valk, CEO, TinQwise
What evolved from this session

PERMISSION
TO STRUGGLE

The TinQwise session was an early proof point that Timescaping works even in compressed formats. It also surfaced a recurring tension: some participants found the 100-year horizon difficult to hold, either because it felt too far away or because it triggered anxiety.

This led to the introduction of a clearer framing at the start of sessions, explicitly naming the 100 years as a device rather than a prediction, and giving participants permission to struggle with it.

The session also confirmed that the format scales down as well as up, which opened thinking about shorter formats for different entry points.
